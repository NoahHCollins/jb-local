Continuum mechanics equations#

Continuum mechanical modelling involves solving one or more of a set of equations that capture:

  1. Kinematics - describing deformation and velocity without considering forces

  2. Dynamics - describing the balance of forces, i.e., conservation of linear and angular momentum

  3. Thermodynamics - describing the relation between temperature, heatflux, stress, entropy

  4. Constitutive equations - describing how material properties vary as a function of temperature, pressure, stress,…

The main set of equations are:

  1. Conservation of mass

  2. Conservation of momentum (Newton’s second law for linear momentum)

  3. Conservation of energy (First law of thermodynamics)

Some problems can be addressed by solving one of these equations alone (e.g. the conservation of energy to solve how heat transfers conductively through an object heated from one side), or a coupled set of equations (e.g. flow of a hot fluid through a porous medium by jointly solving conservation of mass, momentum and energy).

If material properties vary with any of the primary parameters, then additional equations, describing for example viscosity or thermal conductivity, need to be added.

The continuity equation (conservation of mass)#

The continuity equation captures the requirement that no mass is lost during deformation or flow.

As discussed in the lecture, if material is incompressible, i.e. its density does not change during flow or deformation, this equation reduces to the form:

\[\nabla\cdot\v = 0 \]

If the density changes in time and/or space, additional terms need to be included, yielding the following equation:

in spatial description:

\[\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla\cdot(\rho\v) = 0 .\]

or in material description:

\[\frac{D\rho}{Dt} + \rho\nabla\cdot\v = 0 .\]

Exercise 1#

Given the following 3D velocity field,

\[v_i=\frac{kx_i}{(1+kt)}, \]

where \(k\) is a constant, find the density of a material particle as a function of time; i.e., derive \(\rho(t)\).

Answer 1#

Use the continuity equation in the material description

Calculating the divergence of velocity for our 3D velocity field:

\[\nabla\cdot\v = \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_i} = \frac{k}{(1+kt)} \frac{\partial x_i}{\partial x_i}\]
\[\nabla\cdot\v = \frac{k}{(1+kt)} \left(\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial x_1} + \frac{\partial x_2}{\partial x_2} +\frac{\partial x_3}{\partial x_3} \right)\]
\[\nabla\cdot\v = \frac{3k}{(1+kt)}\]

Hence,

\[\frac{D\rho}{Dt} = -\frac{3k\rho}{(1+kt)} .\]

Integrating with respect to time from \(\rho(0) = \rho_0\) to \(\rho(t)\):

\[\int_{\rho_0}^{\rho} \frac{d\rho'}{\rho'} = \int_0^t \frac{-3k}{(1+kt')} dt'\]
\[\ln \rho - \ln\rho_0 = -3 \ln(1+kt)\]
\[\ln \left(\frac{\rho}{\rho_0}\right) = \ln(1+kt)^{-3}\]
\[\rho(t) = \frac{\rho_0}{(1+kt)^3} .\]

This implies that the density of the particle decreases with time as it moves through the flow. Going back to the (2D version of) the same velocity field from the first example, this is apparent from the fact that shapes expand in area as they move through the flow.

The equation of motion and force equilibrium#

In the lecture we saw how taking a force balance acting on an infinitesimal cube in a continuum results in a partial differential equation that describes the conservation of momentum,

\[\rho\frac{\partial^2 \u}{\partial t^2} = \vector{f} + \nabla\cdot\tensor{\sigma} \]

or

\[ \rho\frac{\partial \v}{\partial t} = \vector{f} + \nabla\cdot\tensor{\sigma} .\]

The term on the left-hand side is the mass (per unit volume) times the acceleration; the terms on the right-hand side are therefore the sum of the forces (per unit mass) acting on the body, which for each coordinate direction include the net body force (per unit mass) \(\vector{f}\) and the sum of the stress gradients \(\nabla\cdot\tensor{\sigma}\) acting in that direction.

This implies that if the continuum is in equilibrium at a point (i.e., no acceleration), the body force and stress gradients must be in exact balance in each direction.

If there is no body force then the sum of the stress gradients in each coordinate direction must be zero.

Exercise 2#

Consider the following stress distribution:

\[\begin{split}\tensor{\sigma} = \left[\begin{array}{ccc} x_1 + x_2 & \sigma_{12}(x_1, x_2) & 0 \\ \sigma_{12}(x_1, x_2) & x_1 - 2x_2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & x_2 \end{array} \right] ,\end{split}\]

where \(\sigma_{12}\) is a linear function of \(x_1\) and \(x_2\).

Find \(\sigma_{12}\) given that the stress vector on plane \(x_1 = 1\) is given by:

\[\vector{t} = (1+x_2)\ex + (5-x_2)\ey ,\]

and that the stress distribution is in equilibrium with zero body force.

Answer 2#

Let \(\sigma_{12}\) be a linear function of \(x_1\) and \(x_2\):

\[\sigma_{12} = ax_1 + bx_2 + c ,\]

where \(a\), \(b\) and \(c\) are constants. The plane \(x_1 = 1\) has a normal of \(\ex = (1, 0, 0)\). Hence, the force on this plane is given by:

\[\vector{t} = \tensor{\sigma}^T \cdot \ex .\]
\[\begin{split}\vector{t} = \left[\begin{array}{ccc} x_1 + x_2 & \sigma_{12}(x_1, x_2) & 0 \\ \sigma_{12}(x_1, x_2) & x_1 - 2x_2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & x_2 \end{array} \right]\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{array} \right] = \left[\begin{array}{c} x_1 + x_2 \\ ax_1 + bx_2 + c \\ 0 \end{array} \right].\end{split}\]

Equating this with \(\vector{t}\) given in the question for \(x_1 = 1\):

\[\begin{split}\vector{t} = \left[\begin{array}{c} 1 + x_2 \\ 5 - x_2 \\ 0 \end{array} \right] = \left[\begin{array}{c} 1 + x_2 \\ a + bx_2 + c \\ 0 \end{array} \right].\end{split}\]

By inspection we see that \(b = -1\) and \(a+c = 5\). Hence, we have:

\[\begin{split}\tensor{\sigma} = \left[\begin{array}{ccc} x_1 + x_2 & ax_1 - x_2 + c & 0 \\ ax_1 - x_2 + c & x_1 - 2x_2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & x_2 \end{array} \right] \end{split}\]

Given the stress state is in equilibrium with no body forces, we can also use the constraint that \(\frac{\partial\sigma_{ij}}{\partial x_j} = 0\) (\(\nabla\cdot\tensor{\sigma} = \vector{0}\)).

Consider the following stress distribution:

In the \(x_1\) direction:

\[\frac{\partial\sigma_{11}}{\partial x_1} + \frac{\partial\sigma_{21}}{\partial x_2} + \frac{\partial\sigma_{31}}{\partial x_3} = 1 - 1 + 0 = 0 ;\]

in the \(x_2\) direction:

\[\frac{\partial\sigma_{12}}{\partial x_1} + \frac{\partial\sigma_{22}}{\partial x_2} + \frac{\partial\sigma_{32}}{\partial x_3} = a - 2 + 0 = 0, \]

which implies \(a=2\) (and \(c = 5-2 = 3\)); and in the \(x_3\) direction:

\[\frac{\partial\sigma_{13}}{\partial x_1} + \frac{\partial\sigma_{23}}{\partial x_2} + \frac{\partial\sigma_{33}}{\partial x_3} = 0 + 0 + 0 = 0. \]

Hence, we have the result:

\[\sigma_{12}(x_1, x_2) = 2x_1 - x_2 + 3 .\]

Conservation of Energy#

The general form of the conservation of energy equation that we considered in the lecture is:

\[\frac{D\rho C_pT}{Dt} = \nabla\cdot k\nabla T + A + \tensor{\sigma}:\tensor{D} ,\]

where, from left to right, the different terms represent:

  1. change in temperature with time

  2. heat transfer by conduction (and radiation)

  3. heat production (including latent heat)

  4. heat generated by internal deformation

1D Advection-Diffusion#

In the lecture, we saw how the conservation of energy equation simplifies to the 1-D steady-state advection-diffusion equation in the absence of spatial variation in material parameters, internal deformation and heat production \(A\):

\[\kappa {\frac {\partial ^2 T}{\partial z^2}}+ v_z\frac{\partial T}{\partial z} = 0\]

where:

  • \(T\) is temperature (in \(^{\circ}\)C)

  • \(z\) is depth (in m)

  • \(\kappa = k/(\rho C_p)\) is thermal diffusivity (in m\(^2\)/s)

  • \(v_z\) vertical velocity (in m/s)

Exercise 4#

(a) Write down the energy equation for a 1-D steady state problem with internal heat production, but without any contribution of strain or flow.

(b) Solve this equation for a layer with constant material properties, with a fixed temperature on the bottom and an insulating top:

\[T(y=0)=T_0, \ \ \ \frac{dT}{dy}(y=h)=0.\]

(c) Plot the solution for the following parameter values: \(T_0 = 0^\circ\)C, \(k = 80\) Wm\(^{-1}\)K\(^{-1}\), \(A = 200\) Wm\(^{-3}\), \(h = 2\) m.

Answer 4#

(a) The full energy equation is: $\(\frac{\partial\rho C_pT}{\partial t} + \vector{v}\cdot\nabla{\rho C_pT}= \nabla\cdot k\nabla T + A + \tensor{\sigma}:\tensor{D} ,\)$

  • Steady state means \(\frac{D}{Dt}=0\)

  • No flow means \(\vector{v}=\)

  • No strain means \(\tensor{\sigma}:\tensor{D} = 0\)

So, for this case, the equation reduces to: $\(\nabla\cdot k\nabla T + A = 0\)\( where \)k\( is conductivity and \)A$ is heat production.

In 1-D this becomes: $\(\frac{d}{dy} k\frac{dT}{dy} + A = 0\)$

(b) Coordinate \(y\) = 0 at the base of the domain and \(h\) at the top of the domain.

For constant \(k\), the equation can be further rewritten as: $\(k\frac{d^2T}{dy^2} + A = 0\)\( \)\(\frac{d^2T}{dy^2} = -\frac{A}{k}\)$

Solve this by integrating once: $\(\frac{dT}{dy} = -\frac{Ay}{k} + C_1\)$

Constant \(C_1\) can be found by using the boundary condition: \(\frac{dT}{dy}(y=h) = 0\)

\[0 = -\frac{A}{k}h + C_1 \ \ \Rightarrow \ \ C_1 = \frac{A}{k}h .\]

Integrating again, gives:

\[T(y) = -\frac{A}{2k}y^2 + C_1y + C_2 = -\frac{A}{k}y^2 + \frac{A}{k}hy + C_2.\]

Using the boundary condition: \(T(0) = T_0\) gives:

\[T_0 = 0 + 0 + C_2 \ \ \Rightarrow C_2 = T_0.\]

Putting this altogether:

\[T(y) = T_0 + \frac{A}{2k}y(2h-y).\]

Conservation of Momentum: Hookean Elasticity#

In the lecture, we saw how combining the conservation of momentum equation with the constitutive equation for an elastic material leads to the elastic wave equation.

For an isotropic Hookean solid, the constitutive equation only involves two elastic parameters, taking the following form when cast in terms of the two Lamé constants: $\(\sigma_{ij}=\lambda \epsilon_{kk} \delta_{ij} + 2 \mu \epsilon_{ij}\)$

Writing out the missing steps in the derivation in the lecture slides is a good practise of tensor and index notation skills

Remembering that the conservation of momentum, in index notation is: $\(\rho\frac{\partial^2 u_{i}}{\partial t^2} = f_{i} + \frac{\partial{\sigma_{ji}}}{\partial{x_{j}}}\)$

With the above elastic constitutive equation, the divergence of the stress tensor is: $$ \frac{\partial{\sigma_{ji}}}{\partial{x_{j}}} = \lambda \frac{\partial{(\partial{u_{k}}/\partial{x_{k}})}}{\partial{x_{i}}}

  • \mu \frac{\partial^2 u_i}{\partial

  • \mu \frac{\partial{(\partial{u_{j}}/\partial{x_{j}})}}{\partial{x_{i}}}$$

Or in vector form $\(\nabla\cdot\vector{\sigma}=(\lambda+\mu)\nabla(\nabla\cdot\vector{u})+\mu\nabla^2\vector{u}\)$

Using the relation: $\(\nabla^2\vector{u}=\nabla(\nabla\cdot\vector{u})-\nabla\times\nabla\times\vector{u}\)$

This yields the following elastic wave equation:

\[\rho\frac{\partial^2\vector{u}}{\partial t^2} = \vector{f} + (\lambda+2\mu)\nabla(\nabla\cdot\vector{u}) - \mu \nabla\times\nabla\times\vector{u}\]

The term with \(\nabla\cdot\vector{u}\) describes changes in volume due to the displacement field and is related to compressional waves, while the term with \(\nabla\times\vector{u}\) describes the rotational component of the displacement field and is related to shear waves.

Exercise 5#

Two alternative elastic parameters often used are the bulk modulus \(K\) and shear modulus \(G\). These two moduli are linked, respectively, to isotropic stress (i.e. pressure \(p\)) and strain (\(\theta\)): $\( -p = \frac{\sigma_{kk}}{3} = K \epsilon_{kk} = K \theta \)$

and deviatoric stress \(\sigma'_{ij}\) and strain \(\epsilon'_{ij}\): $\( \sigma'_{ij} = \sigma_{ij} + p \delta_{ij} = 2 G \epsilon'_{ij} \)$

Derive the expressions relating \(K\) and \(G\) to the Lamé constants \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\)

Answer 5#

For the bulk modulus \(K\): $\(-p = \frac{\sigma_{kk}}{3} = \frac{\lambda \epsilon_{kk} \delta_{kk} + 2 \mu \epsilon_{kk}}{3} = \frac{(3 \lambda +2 \mu )\epsilon_{kk}}{3} = (\lambda +\frac{2}{3} \mu ) \theta \)\( \)\(\Rightarrow K = \lambda +\frac{2}{3} \mu \)$

For the shear modulus \(G\): $\( \sigma'_{ij} = \sigma_{ij} + p \delta_{ij} = 2 G \epsilon'_{ij} \)\( \)\( \sigma'_{ij} = (\lambda \theta \delta_{ij} + 2 \mu \epsilon_{ij}) - (\lambda +\frac{2}{3} \mu ) \theta \delta_{ij} \)\( \)\( \sigma'_{ij} = 2 \mu \epsilon_{ij} - \frac{2}{3} \mu \theta \delta_{ij} = 2 \mu (\epsilon_{ij} - \frac{\theta}{3} \delta_{ij})\)$

As deviatoric strain \(\epsilon'_{ij} = \epsilon_{ij} - \frac{\theta}{3} \delta_{ij} \), this means: $\(\sigma'_{ij} = 2 \mu \epsilon'_{ij} \)\( \)\(\Rightarrow G = \mu \)$

Note that the term in the wave equation that pertains to compressional waves is scaled by \((\lambda + 2\mu)\), i.e. a parameter closely related to \(K\), while the term that pertains to shear waves is scaled by the shear modulus \(G\).

(b) Find a relation between the principal values of stress and strain using the two Lamé parameters.

(c) For the same Hookean solid, express the elastic Young’s modulus \(E\) and Poisson’s ratio \(\nu\), which are often used in engineering, in terms of the Lamé parameters \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\).

The two engineering moduli are defined for a uniaxial state of stress, where \(\sigma_1 \ne 0\) and \(\sigma_2 = \sigma_3 = 0\), a useful system for experimentally determining the elastic parameters. This stress leads to a maximum strain \(\epsilon_1\) in the direction of the applied stress and uniform strain in perpendicular direction, \(\epsilon_2 = \epsilon_3\).

The moduli are then defined as:

\[E=\sigma_1/\epsilon_1 \ \ \text{and} \ \ \nu= -\epsilon_3/\epsilon_1 .\]

Answer 6#

(a) Show that for an isotropic Hookean solid, principal directions of stress and strain coincide.

For an isotropic Hookean solid, the stress strain relationship is given by:

\[\tensor{\sigma} = \lambda \theta \tensor{I} + 2\mu\tensor{\epsilon} ,\]

where \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\) are the Lamé parameters and \(\theta\) is the trace of the strain tensor.

Let \(\x_1\) be an eigenvector of the strain tensor \(\tensor{\epsilon}\), with eigenvalue \(c_1\), such that:

\[\tensor{\epsilon}\cdot\x_1 = c_1\x_1 .\]

Multiplying Hooke’s law through be \(\x_1\) gives:

\[\tensor{\sigma}\cdot\x_1 = \lambda \theta \tensor{I}\cdot\x_1 + 2\mu\tensor{\epsilon}\cdot\x_1 ,\]

which implies that:

\[\tensor{\sigma}\cdot\x_1 = \lambda \theta \x_1 + 2\mu c_1 \x_1 ,\]
\[\tensor{\sigma}\cdot\x_1 = \left(\lambda \theta + 2\mu c_1\right) \x_1 .\]

Hence, \(\x_1\) is also an eigenvector of \(\tensor{\sigma}\), with eignevalue \(\lambda \theta + 2\mu c_1\).

(b) Find a relation between the principal values of stress and strain using the two Lamé parameters.

The relationship between principal stress and strain components is:

\[\begin{split}\begin{pmatrix} \sigma_1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma_2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \sigma_3 \end{pmatrix} = \lambda (\epsilon_1 + \epsilon_2 + \epsilon_3) \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} + 2\mu \begin{pmatrix} \epsilon_1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \epsilon_2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \epsilon_3 \end{pmatrix} .\end{split}\]

This implies that for individual components:

\[\sigma_1 = \lambda\theta + 2\mu\epsilon_1\]
\[\sigma_2 = \lambda\theta + 2\mu\epsilon_2\]
\[\sigma_3 = \lambda\theta + 2\mu\epsilon_3\]

Or in index notation:

\[\sigma_i = \lambda\theta + 2\mu\epsilon_i\]

(c) For the same Hookean solid, express the elastic Young’s modulus \(E\) and Poisson’s ratio \(\nu\), which are often used in engineering, in terms of the Lamé parameters \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\).

To answer this, we consider the case of uniaxial stress, for which the principal stresses are: \(\sigma_1 = \sigma_1\), \(\sigma_2 = \sigma_3 = 0\) and the principal strains are: \(\epsilon_1 = \epsilon_1\), \(\epsilon_2 = \epsilon_3\).

Young’s modulus is defined as \(E=\sigma_1/\epsilon_1\).

Poisson’s ratio is defined as \(\nu= -\epsilon_3/\epsilon_1\).

Hooke’s law for the third principal stress:

\[\sigma_3 = \lambda(\epsilon_1 + 2\epsilon_3) + 2\mu\epsilon_3 = 0 .\]

This equation implies:

\[-\lambda\epsilon_1 = 2(\lambda + \mu)\epsilon_3\]
\[\frac{-\lambda}{\lambda + \mu}\epsilon_1 = 2\epsilon_3\]
\[\nu = -\frac{\epsilon_3}{\epsilon_1} = \frac{\lambda}{2(\lambda + \mu)} .\]

Hooke’s law for the first principal stress:

\[\sigma_1 = \lambda(\epsilon_1 + 2\epsilon_3) + 2\mu\epsilon_1\]

Using the result from earlier that \(2\epsilon_3 = \frac{-\lambda}{\lambda + \mu}\epsilon_1\):

\[\sigma_1 = \lambda\left(\epsilon_1 + \frac{-\lambda}{\lambda + \mu}\epsilon_1\right) + 2\mu\epsilon_1\]
\[\sigma_1 = \left(\lambda - \frac{\lambda^2}{\lambda + \mu} + 2\mu\right) \epsilon_1\]

Which implies:

\[E = \frac{\sigma_1}{\epsilon_1} = \lambda - \frac{\lambda^2}{\lambda + \mu} + 2\mu = \frac{(\lambda + 2\mu)(\lambda + \mu) - \lambda^2}{\lambda + \mu}\]

Or

\[E = \frac{\mu(3\lambda + 2\mu)}{\lambda + \mu}\]

Conservation of Momentum: Navier-Stokes#

The lecture discussed that combining the conservation of momentum equation with the constitutive equation for an viscous material leads to the so-called Navier-Stokes equation for viscous flow and deformation.

To consider this case, we decomposed the stress tensor, \(\vector{\sigma}\), into an isotropic part, \(p \vector{I}\), and a deviatoric part, \(\matrix{\sigma'}\).

Hydrostatic state of stress

The isotropic part describes the hydrostatic state of fluids at rest, as fluids are not able to sustain shear stresses. If a fluid is at rest, the force balance reduces to: $\(\nabla \cdot \vector{\sigma} + \vector{f} = -\nabla p + \vector{f} = \vector{0} ~~~~\Rightarrow~~~~\nabla p = \vector{f}\)$

Navier-Stokes for a Newtonian fluid

In the case of a Newtonian fluid, deviatoric stress, \(\vector{\sigma'}\), varies linearly with strain rate, \(\vector{D}\). Similarly to the elastic case, if the material is isotropic, there are only two viscosity parameters: bulk viscosity, \(\zeta\), and shear viscosity, \(\eta\). The deviatoric stress tensor (in index notation) is then given by: $\(\sigma'_{ij}= \zeta D_{kk} \delta_{ij} + 2\eta D_{ij}\)$

If we define \(\Delta = D_{kk} = \nabla \cdot \vector{v}\), then the full expression for the stress tensor for an isotropic, Newtonian fluid is: $\(\vector{\sigma} = (-p + \zeta\Delta)\vector{I} + 2\eta \vector{D}\)$

Putting this expression into the force balance, $\(\nabla \cdot \vector{\sigma} + \vector{f} = \rho \frac{D\vector{v}}{Dt}\)\( assuming that \)\zeta\( and \)\eta\( are constant yields: \)\(-\nabla p + (\zeta + \eta) \nabla \Delta + \eta \nabla^{2} \vector{v} + \vector{f} = \rho \frac{D\vector{v}}{Dt} = \rho (\frac{\partial{\vector{v}}}{\partial{t}} + \vector{v} \cdot \nabla \vector{v})\)$

Navier-Stokes for an incompressible, Newtonian fluid

If the material is incompressible, then \(\Delta = \nabla \cdot \vector{v} = 0\), and the equation reduces to:

\[-\nabla p + \eta \nabla^{2} \vector{v} + \vector{f} = \rho (\frac{\partial{\vector{v}}}{\partial{t}} + \vector{v} \cdot \nabla \vector{v})\]

Navier-Stokes + continuity equations

In 3-D, this Navier-Stokes equations above correspond to three equations (force balance in \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\) direction) and has four unknowns: pressure \(p\), and the three components of velocity \(\vector{v}=(v_x,v_y,v_z)\). By solving it together with the continuity equation \(\nabla\cdot\vector{v}=0\), this yields a system of four equations with four unknowns.

Exercise 7#

Consider a Newtonian shear flow with velocity field \(v_1(x_2), v_2=v_3=0\).

Write out all components of the strain rate tensor D and the stres tensor \(\vector{\sigma}\).

Answer 7#

Remember that:

\[ D_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} (\frac{\partial{v_i}}{\partial{x_j}} + \frac{\partial{v_j}}{\partial{x_i}})\]

With the given velocity field:

\[D_{11}=D_{22}=D_{33}=0\]
\[D_{12}=D_{21}=\frac{1}{2} \frac{d v_1}{d x_2}\]
\[D_{13}=D_{31}=D_{23}=D_{32}=0\]

Because \(\nabla \cdot \vector{v} = 0\): $\(\sigma_{ij} = -p \delta_{ij} + 2 \eta D_{ij}\)$

Therefore: $\(\sigma_{11}=\sigma_{22}=\sigma_{33}=-p\)\( \)\(\sigma_{12}=\sigma_{21}=\eta \frac{1}{2} \frac{d v_1}{d x_2}\)\( \)\(\sigma_{13}=\sigma_{31}=\sigma_{23}=\sigma_{32}=0\)$

This example illustrates that \(\eta\) represents the resistance to shearing.

Exercise 8#

Consider the case of steady unidirectional flow of an incompressible fluid with constant viscosity \(\eta\) between two parallel fixed plates, both with a normal pointing in the \(x_2\) direction. Assume flow is in the \(x_1\) direction, and the plates extend infinitely in the \(x_1\) and \(x_3\) directions (i.e., the distance between the plates is much smaller than the size of the plates). This type of flow is called plane Poiseuille flow. The velocity field for this flow has the form:

\[v_1=v(x_2), \ \ v_2 = v_3 =0.\]

(a) Write down the Navier-Stokes equations for this problem.

(b) Show that the pressure gradient is constant in the direction of flow. I.e., that:

\[\frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial x_1^2}=0. \]

(c) With this information, find the solution for \(v(x_2)\), assuming the plates are located at \(x_2 = -b\) and \(x_2=b\).

(d) What does the constant \(c\) correspond to in the solution? Use matplotlib to plot the shapes of solutions for various ratios of \(c/\eta\).

Answer 8#

(a)

Since the flow is steady state and there are no external forces we have that the velocity field can be written

\[ \frac{\mathrm{D}\mathbf{v}}{\mathrm{D}t}=0, \qquad \mathbf{F}=0 \]

Further, we are given that

\[\mathbf{v}=v(x_2)\mathbf{e}_1+0\mathbf{e}_2+0\mathbf{e}_3\]

Substituting these conditions into the Navier Stokes equations we are left with

\[ -\nabla p + \eta\nabla^{2}\mathbf{v}=0\]

However, owing to the form of the velocity field this further reduces to, in the \(x_1\) direction:

\[ -\frac{\partial p}{\partial x_1} + \eta\frac{\partial^2 v(x_2)}{\partial x_2^2}=0\]

Note that in the \(x_2\) and \(x_3\) directions we have that \(\partial p/\partial x_2=0\) and \(\partial p/\partial x_3=0\), i.e. \(p=p(x_1)\).

(b)

From part (a) we have that \(p=p(x_1)\) and that

\[ -\frac{\partial p(x_1)}{\partial x_1} + \eta\frac{\partial^2 v(x_2)}{\partial x_2^2}=0\]

Differentiating this expression with respect to \(x_1\) to get the pressure gradient we have

\[ -\frac{\partial^2 p(x_1)}{\partial x_1^2} + \eta\frac{\partial}{\partial x_1}\frac{\partial^2 v(x_2)}{\partial x_2^2}=0\]

However, since our velocity is only a function of \(x_2\)

\[\frac{\partial v(x_2)}{\partial x_1}=0\]

Hence

\[ \frac{\partial^2 p(x_1)}{\partial x_1^2}=0 \]

(c)

Integrating our pressure equation with respect to \(x_1\) we have that

\[\frac{\partial p(x_1)}{\partial x_1} = -c\]

Where \(c\) is some constant and note that we have chosen our right hand side to be \(-c\) since the flow will move in the direction of decreasing pressure (i.e. we know \(c\) will now be some positive constant). We can then substitute this into our Navier stokes equation to obtain

\[c+\eta\frac{\partial^2 v(x_2)}{\partial x_2^2}=0\]

or

\[\frac{\partial^2 v(x_2)}{\partial x_2^2}=-\frac{c}{\eta}\]

Integrating twice with respect to \(x_2\) we have that

\[ v(x_2)=-\frac{c}{2\eta}x_2^2+B_1 x + B_2\]

where \(B_1\) and \(B_2\) are our new constants of integration. To evaluate the constants \(B_1\) and \(B_2\) we now need to apply our boundary conditions which are given as

\[v(b)=v(-b)=0\]

The first condition, \(v(b)=0\), gives

\[-\frac{c}{2\eta}b^2+B_1 b + B_2=0\]

the second, \(v(-b)=0\), gives

\[-\frac{c}{2\eta}b^2-B_1 b + B_2=0\]

Solving these two simultaneous equations yields

\[B_1 = 0, \qquad B_2=\frac{c}{2\eta}b^2\]

Hence, our final solution can be written as

$\(v(x_2)=\frac{c}{2\eta}(b-x_2^2)\)$.

Note that our flow is thus parabolic!

(d)

The constant \(c\) corresponds to the strength of the far-field pressure source (that’s forcing the flow in the \(x_1\) direction). Hence (for a fixed viscosity), the larger this force, the larger the peak velocity.

Below we plot the velocity field for ratios of \(c/\eta\) between 0 and 2. For plotting purposes, we will choose \(b=1\).

# Function defining our velocity
def vel(x2, coeta, b=1):
    return 0.5*(coeta)*(b-x2**2)

b = 1
dx2 = 0.01
x2 = np.linspace(-b, b, int(2*b/dx2+1))

# Compute velocity profiles for various c/eta
v01 = vel(x2, 0.1)
v05 = vel(x2, 0.5)
v10 = vel(x2, 1.0)
v15 = vel(x2, 1.5)
v20 = vel(x2, 2.0)

# Initialise set up for figure
tempfig, ax= plt.subplots(1)
tempfig.suptitle("Various $x_2$ velocity profiles")
linecol=['k-','b-','c-','g-','r-']
plt.xlabel("$v(x_2)$")
plt.ylabel("$x_2$")

# Some horizontal lines at +,- b
plt.axhline(y=-b, color='k', linestyle='-')
plt.axhline(y=b, color='k', linestyle='-')

# Velocity profiles
plt.plot(v01,x2,label="0.1")
plt.plot(v05,x2,label="0.5")
plt.plot(v10,x2,label="1.0")
plt.plot(v15,x2,label="1.5")
plt.plot(v20,x2,label="2.0")
plt.legend(title='$\eta/c$')
